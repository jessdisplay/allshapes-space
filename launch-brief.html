05 · Demo programme

Six demos to program

Each one is real hospitality for the guests in the room and a repeatable demo Wonder Byte can sell afterwards — they're the same "robot experiences" priced on the hire page.

1

Voice-first welcome

Cruzr · every guest, designed for blind & low-vision guests

Cruzr greets at the heritage entry with voice before screen — name of the event, what's where, and “say 'guide me' and I'll walk you in.” Nothing requires touching or seeing a screen; the screen mirrors everything said in large print.

FeasibilityCruzr's SDK exposes ASR/speech, face detection and navigation-to-coordinates; multilingual voice is stock capability.
References: Cruzr SDK guide
2

Guided escort along the spine

Cruzr · blind & low-vision guests

An audible escort from entry to any station: the robot self-announces continuously so it can be tracked by ear, announces arrivals, and stop/resume works by voice alone — no app. Research with blind users shows silent robots are the single biggest failure; this demo is the fix, made visible.

FeasibilityNamed map points + scripted announcements per waypoint via the Cruzr navigation API. Modelled on the AI Suitcase (voice in, audio out) and AccessibilityOz's 'do less, reliably' principles.
References: Blind users & service robots (CSCW) · AI Suitcase · Guide dogs to guide robots
3

Canapés at every height

Cadebot · wheelchair users first, everyone served

Cadebot's three trays become a feature: middle tray announced as the wheelchair-first tray, contents named per tray on arrival, slow approach, stop offset so it never blocks a wheelchair's path, and it waits — leaving only when tray sensors confirm items were taken or a generous dwell expires. At toast time: the cake run — one rehearsed hero route carrying the birthday cake, announced in audio and on screen so the whole room knows the moment is coming.

FeasibilityCadebot ships with modular trays, NLP voice, and a LiDAR + RGBD + ultrasonic suite; routes, announcements and dwell are managed through UBTECH's CARS system and VAPP app. Stop-position and redundancy requirements come from CHI 2024 co-design with wheelchair users.
References: Cadebot specs · CHI 2024 co-design study
4

Coffee, ordered by voice

Coffee arm · everyone, designed for blind guests

Order entirely by voice, hear the machine narrate its progress, get called by name at hand-off — with the cup placed at a consistent, announced position so a blind guest can find it without touching hot equipment.

FeasibilityVoice-ordering robot baristas are shipping products; Wonder Byte's arm already runs live production at the Hub, so the work is the ordering/announcement layer, not the coffee.
References: Voice robot barista (ABC7) · Robot bar service in care settings
5

Say it, show it, light it

Whole fleet · redundant communication

Every announcement is triple-coded: voice, large-print high-contrast text on the robot's screen, and a light cue. Deaf and hard-of-hearing guests get everything visually; blind guests get everything audibly; nobody gets less.

FeasibilityCadebot carries 21.5″ + 10.1″ displays and Cruzr an 11.6″ touchscreen — screen content is developer-controllable via the UBTECH stack. Redundancy requirement straight from the HRI accessibility guidelines and CHI findings.
References: HRI accessibility guidelines · Guidelines repo
6

Smart disobedience

Whole fleet · safety as theatre

A staged moment for the industry crowd: a robot mid-route meets a white cane / a wheelchair crossing / a guest saying “move away” — and stops, announces, re-routes. Yielding is programmed behaviour, and disabled guests can command it.

FeasibilityObstacle detection (LiDAR + RGBD + ultrasonic, 15 mm obstacle / glass detection on Cadebot) is stock; the demo work is the voice-override intent and announcement scripts — a direct ask from wheelchair users in the CHI 2024 study.
References: CHI 2024 co-design study · Cadebot sensor specs

The one to talk about

A pilot in the room who isn't in the room

Tokyo's DAWN Avatar Robot Café staffs its floor with robots piloted remotely — including by eye-tracking — by people with ALS, SMA and other disabilities. If one station at our launch were piloted live by a remote host with disability, the event stops being "robots serving disabled guests" and becomes what All Shapes is actually about: disabled people running the show, through robots. Worth asking Gino which unit could support tele-operation.

Co-designing Accessible Public Robots — CHI 2024 ↗

Wheelchair users asked for: visible robot height, stop positions that never block paths, redundant communication, explicit signals before speed changes, and a spoken 'move away' override. Demos 3, 5 and 6 are built from this.

“I was confused by it; it was confused by me” — CSCW 2022 ↗

17 blind/low-vision interviewees: robots without audio feedback are impossible to locate and frightening when they approach silently. Motivates the continuous self-announcement in Demos 2–3.
